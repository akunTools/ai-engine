How the Math Works

Logo Churn Rate = (Customers Lost ÷ Customers at Start) × 100. This tells you what proportion of your customer base canceled this month.

Gross MRR Churn Rate = (MRR Lost ÷ MRR at Start) × 100. This is the raw revenue leakage before factoring in upgrades.

Net MRR Churn Rate = ((MRR Lost − Expansion MRR) ÷ MRR at Start) × 100. When expansion MRR exceeds lost MRR, you achieve negative net churn — a holy grail for SaaS.

Annual Revenue at Risk = MRR Lost × 12. The dollar amount you stand to lose over a full year if this month’s churn pattern continues unchanged.

What Is a Healthy Churn Rate for SaaS?

The short answer: for a bootstrapped SaaS aiming at sustainable growth, monthly logo churn under 2% is healthy, 2–4% is a warning sign, and above 5% means your business model is on fire. These are rough benchmarks, and they shift depending on your ACV (average contract value) and market. A micro-SaaS charging $9/month can tolerate slightly higher churn than a $200/month B2B tool, but the trend matters more than a single monthly tick.

Net MRR churn paints an even sharper picture. If your expansion MRR from existing customers (upgrades, add-ons, seat expansion) outweighs lost MRR, your net churn goes negative. That’s where compounding magic happens. Many high-growth, bootstrapped SaaS companies like ConvertKit and Baremetrics have publicly celebrated negative net churn because it means their existing customer base alone grows revenue — before a single new sign-up.

I remember the first time my MRR churn dropped below 2%. It wasn’t a coincidence — we had spent six months obsessing over onboarding and customer success. The numbers gave us permission to scale acquisition without fear. If you’re seeing churn above 3% right now, don’t panic. Use that anger to fuel the retention work. How to Reduce Churn in the First 90 Days

The first 90 days after signup are where most churn happens — and where you have the most leverage to stop it. Customers who leave early rarely complain about missing features; they leave because they never experienced the core value. Your activation flow, onboarding emails, and first-week engagement are your churn levers.

We once cut 30-day churn by 26% simply by adding a 2‑minute onboarding video and a single human-touch email on day two. That change alone added $14,400 to ARR over the next year, based on an $8,000 MRR starting point. The math works the other way too: let’s say you have $15k MRR with a 5% monthly gross MRR churn. That’s $750 in lost MRR each month — $9,000/year. Reducing churn to 3% saves you $3,600/year without a single new customer. That’s the power of retention.

Quick wins:

  • Track time-to-value (TTV) for every signup. If the average user takes three days to hit their “aha” moment, redesign your flow to make it one.
  • Implement a health score for trialing accounts based on key actions.
  • Reach out personally to every customer who cancels in the first two weeks — you’ll learn more in five conversations than in a hundred NPS surveys.

The Hidden Cost of Churn: Annual Revenue at Risk

Most founders think of churn as a percentage, but that number hides the dollar impact. The “Annual Revenue at Risk” metric in the calculator above is designed to break that illusion. Take a scenario: 400 customers at the start of the month, 16 lost — that’s 4% logo churn, which feels manageable. But if your monthly MRR lost is $1,200, your annual revenue at risk is $14,400. Over three years, the compounded cost of that churn (factoring in foregone expansion and referral value) easily exceeds $50,000. For a bootstrapped SaaS where every dollar comes from customer cash, that’s a founder’s salary or a critical hire.

Another concrete scenario: $20k MRR, losing $1,500/month in churned MRR = $18,000/year at risk. If your gross margins are 80%, you’re burning $14,400 in profit annually. Now compare that to your customer acquisition cost. If you spend $3,000 to acquire each new customer and churn is high, you’re paying a tax that no amount of content marketing can outrun.

SaaS Churn Metrics That Actually Matter (And Those That Don’t)

The industry loves acronyms, but three metrics do the heavy lifting: logo churn rate, gross MRR churn, and net MRR churn. Customer satisfaction scores and NPS are useful signals, but they’re trailing indicators. Churn is a leading indicator of whether your product truly solves a painful problem.

Logo churn tells you about product/market fit and customer experience. Gross MRR churn exposes revenue concentration risk — if you lose a few high-paying accounts, it spikes. Net MRR churn shows whether your existing base is an engine or an anchor. If you’re tracking all three, you can diagnose problems with surgical precision. High logo churn but low MRR churn? Your cheapest plans are leaky, but enterprise accounts stick. The opposite pattern means you’re losing big accounts but keeping small ones — a dangerous slope.

One metric I ignore completely in isolation: “churn by cohort without dollar context.” It can mislead you into celebrating a high-volume cohort that’s actually destroying value. Instead, always view churn through the lens of revenue impact. The numbers in the calculator above make that reflex automatic.

Frequently Asked Questions

What’s the difference between logo churn and MRR churn?

Logo churn counts individual customers who leave, while MRR churn measures the recurring revenue lost through cancellations and downgrades. A single large customer churning can cause high MRR churn even if logo churn is low — both matter, and the calculator above shows them side by side.

Is negative net MRR churn possible for bootstrapped SaaS?

Absolutely. If your expansion revenue (upgrades, add-ons, cross-sells) from existing customers exceeds the MRR lost to cancellations, you achieve negative net churn. Bootstrapped companies like Buffer and Basecamp have publicly shared this achievement, and it’s a powerful signal that your product creates more value over time.

How often should I recalculate churn?

Monthly, without exception. Churn is a leading indicator that changes fast. Review these numbers every month during your founder review, and run the calculator in under a minute. Tracking trends over rolling 3‑ and 6‑month windows helps smooth out one-off anomalies.

My churn is below 2% — can I ignore retention?

Under 2% monthly is strong, but don’t become complacent. A seemingly healthy churn rate can mask hidden risks like concentration in a few accounts or a stagnant expansion motion. Use the calculator’s net MRR churn and annual revenue at risk to keep pressure on the expansion engine.
