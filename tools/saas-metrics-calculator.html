How These Metrics Are Calculated

MRR Growth Rate = (Current MRR – Previous MRR) / Previous MRR × 100

Net New MRR = Current MRR – Previous MRR

ARR = Current MRR × 12

NRR = (Previous MRR + Expansion MRR – Churned MRR) / Previous MRR × 100 (requires expansion and churn data)

Gross MRR Churn Rate = Churned MRR / Previous MRR × 100

Net MRR Churn Rate = (Churned MRR – Expansion MRR) / Previous MRR × 100

Logo Churn Rate = Lost Customers / Starting Customers × 100

ARPU = Current MRR / Active Customers at End

Rule of 40 = MRR Growth Rate % + Profit Margin %

What Is a Healthy MRR Growth Rate for Bootstrapped SaaS?

The question every founder obsesses over, and the answer is brutally context‑dependent. If you’re pre‑product‑market fit, 10–20% monthly growth is table stakes. For a bootstrapped SaaS with $10k–$50k MRR, 8–15% month over month is excellent; 5% is good but needs watching. Once you cross $100k MRR, sustaining 8% monthly becomes nearly impossible without a team — a 3–5% monthly rate is still healthy.

Here’s a realistic benchmark: at $12k MRR, growing 10% means adding $1,200 in new net revenue this month. If your churn eats $800, you need $2,000 in new sales just to hit the target. That’s the math that keeps founders awake at night. Use the calculator to see your real growth rate and then compare it against these tiers:

  • Red flag: Below 3% monthly growth (or negative). You’re treading water.
  • Warning: 3–5% — stable, but not enough to attract attention or reinvest aggressively.
  • Healthy: 5–10% — you’re building momentum, likely with strong retention.
  • Exceptional: 10%+ — usually means you’ve found a repeatable growth engine and customers love the product.

Don’t judge growth in isolation. MRR growth of 7% with a Rule of 40 score of 55% is far healthier than 15% growth with a negative Rule of 40 because you’re burning cash to acquire one‑and‑done users.

How Do You Calculate Net Revenue Retention (NRR)?

NRR is the single most important metric for bootstrapped SaaS that I see founders ignore until it’s too late. It measures how much revenue you keep (and grow) from your existing customer base, ignoring new customers. The formula:

NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion MRR – Churned MRR) / Starting MRR × 100

If you started the month with $20,000 in MRR from existing customers, gained $1,500 from upsells, and lost $1,000 from cancellations, your NRR is ($20,000 + $1,500 – $1,000) / $20,000 = 102.5%. That means your existing customers grew by 2.5% before you even added a new logo.

For bootstrapped founders, an NRR above 100% is magic. It means you can pause new customer acquisition for a month and still grow. Top‑decile public SaaS companies hover around 120% NRR, but for bootstrapped businesses, anything above 100% is a strong signal of product‑market fit. Below 90%, you’re burning cash just to stay still — that’s when I’d drop everything and run a dedicated churn audit immediately.

Notice that NRR uses both expansion and churn MRR. If you only track churn and ignore expansion, you’re missing half the story. Use the calculator: fill in both fields to watch how one can cancel out the other.

The Brutal Truth About Churn: Logo vs. Revenue Churn

Founders often boast “2% monthly churn” when they mean they lost 2% of customers. But if those customers were on the $9/mo plan and your $499/mo anchors stayed, revenue churn might be 0.3%. That’s a very different picture. The calculator gives you both: logo churn (customer count) and gross MRR churn (revenue lost).

Here’s a real scenario: imagine a bootstrapped API tool with 500 customers paying an average of $80/mo. Losing 10 small accounts looks like 2% logo churn — not scary. But if one $500/mo enterprise leaves, gross MRR churn jumps to $500 / $40,000 = 1.25% from just one logo. Logo churn masks revenue risk. I’ve seen founders with “low” 3% logo churn but 8% revenue churn because their largest accounts were walking out the back door.

Rule of thumb for bootstrapped SaaS: keep gross MRR churn below 5% monthly, and aim for 2–3% once you’re past $20k MRR. If your churn rate is above 5%, every dollar you earn starts evaporating within 20 months. The calculator will flag exactly where you stand — and the net MRR churn result tells you whether your expansion efforts are outrunning the leaks.

What's a Good ARPU Target for Bootstrapped SaaS?

Average Revenue Per User (ARPU) isn’t just a vanity metric — it’s the lever that funds your entire growth engine. For bootstrapped SaaS, ARPU must be high enough to sustain a reasonable customer acquisition cost without VC subsidies. A healthy target depends on your market: B2B SaaS tools often aim for $50–$200/mo ARPU, while developer tools or apps might survive on $10–$30/mo if churn is extremely low.

I've seen founders chasing enterprise deals to inflate ARPU while ignoring that a $500/mo customer lost to churn hurts more than ten $50/mo customers. The calculator shows your real ARPU right now — if it’s below $20 and your churn is above 5%, you’re in a death spiral. At $80 ARPU with 3% churn, you’re in great shape to reinvest. Use ARPU alongside NRR: high ARPU with low NRR means you’re not retaining the value you’re creating.

One tactical move: if your ARPU is stagnant, look at expansion revenue before raising prices. Upselling a $15 plan to $25 is easier than acquiring a new customer at $50. The calculator’s expansion MRR field reveals how much growth is already sitting in your base untapped.

Using This Calculator to Diagnose Your SaaS Health

Don’t just punch numbers in — use the results as a monthly diagnostic. I run this calculation every single month for my own SaaS. Here’s the sequence I follow:

  1. Start with MRR Growth Rate. If it’s below 3% and you’re under $50k, stop everything else and find the leak. Is it churn? Pricing? Lead flow? The other metrics will tell you.
  2. Look at NRR. Above 100%? Great, your existing base is healthy. Below 95%? That’s the leak. Dive into expansion and churn — which one is moving against you?
  3. Check Gross MRR Churn. If it’s above 5%, go straight to your pricing and value delivery. You’re likely overcharging for what you deliver, or under‑delivering on onboarding.
  4. Evaluate Logo Churn. If logo churn is high but revenue churn is low, your problem is in the low‑end plans — pricing or activation. If the opposite, your big accounts are unhappy.
  5. Finally, check Rule of 40. A score under 20 tells you growth is costing too much or profitability is non‑existent — both are emergencies. Use this metric when talking to potential investors or even your spouse about why you’re still bootstrapping.

Frequently Asked Questions

What SaaS metrics should I track every month?

At minimum: MRR, MRR growth rate, net revenue retention (NRR), gross MRR churn, and logo churn. Once you’re past $10k MRR, add ARPU and Rule of 40. This calculator covers all of them in one view.

How is the Rule of 40 calculated in this tool?

We use your monthly MRR growth percentage plus your profit margin percentage. While the traditional Rule of 40 uses annual revenue growth, this approximation gives you a fast, directional health check. If your monthly growth is volatile, average three months before running the calculator.

Why does NRR show “—” even though I entered numbers?

NRR needs both expansion MRR and churned MRR fields to be filled. Populate both to see how existing customer revenue is truly changing. If you only have one, gross or net churn rates will still work.
