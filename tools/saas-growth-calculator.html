How the SaaS Growth Calculator Works

MRR Growth Rate: (Current MRR − Previous MRR) ÷ Previous MRR × 100. This is your top-line momentum indicator.

Net New MRR: Current MRR − Previous MRR. Also equals New MRR + Expansion MRR − Churned MRR when those are broken out.

ARR: Current MRR × 12. Gives you the annualized run rate.

NRR: (Previous MRR + Expansion MRR − Churned MRR) ÷ Previous MRR × 100. Shows how your existing customer base is expanding or contracting, net of churn and upsells. A value above 100% means you’re growing even without new logos.

Rule of 40: MRR Growth Rate + Profit Margin (%). A combined health score popularized by venture capital, but equally useful for bootstrapped founders who want to balance growth with free cash flow.

Why MRR Growth Rate Is the Heartbeat of Your Bootstrapped SaaS

Revenue growth is the most tracked number in SaaS, but for bootstrapped founders it’s also the most deceptive. A 10% month-over-month growth rate sounds phenomenal—it implies you’ll triple revenue in a year. But what if that growth comes entirely from one large customer? Or what if every dollar of new MRR is offset by an equal dollar of churn from your existing base?

MRR growth rate tells you the velocity. It doesn’t tell you the torque. That’s why the SaaS Metrics Calculator exists as a companion—to decompose that velocity into its constituent parts: logo churn, expansion, and contraction. But in this growth calculator, we keep it focused. If your growth rate is positive and staying above 5% per month, you’re outpacing most bootstrapped peers. If it dips below 2-3% for multiple months, you’re in the danger zone where runway starts shrinking before your eyes.

Consider a founder at $8k MRR, spending $6k/month on servers, contractors, and marketing. That’s $2k monthly net burn. If MRR growth stalls, the business bleeds cash. A 10% month-over-month growth rate takes them to $8.8k next month, $9.68k after that—still not covering burn by month three. Growth alone doesn’t save you; speed of growth relative to expense does. This calculator—combined with a proper churn rate analysis—shows whether your engine has enough horsepower to clear the cash-flow hill ahead.

What Is a Good Net Revenue Retention (NRR) for Early-Stage SaaS?

Net Revenue Retention is the quiet killer. You can have 20% monthly logo growth and still drown if your NRR is below 80%. Early-stage bootstrapped SaaS founders often ignore NRR because they track logo count. But in the early days, a handful of customers expanding from $50/month to $200/month can mask a 50% logo churn rate. NRR accounts for that by comparing the MRR you started with—plus expansions and minus churn—to your original base.

For a bootstrapped SaaS under $1M ARR, a good NRR target is 90–100%. If you’re selling to small businesses with high churn, 80–90% is still alive—barely. Above 100% means your existing customers are growing faster than they’re leaving, which is the holy grail for capital-efficient companies. I’ve seen a micro-SaaS charging $29/month achieve 115% NRR because 20% of users upgraded to a new Teams plan, more than offsetting the monthly churn of 6%. That business grew from $5k to $12k MRR in 8 months without spending a dime on ads. If your NRR is below 70%, you’re in a leaky bucket; no amount of new customer acquisition will fix it.

When you plug your expansion MRR and churn MRR into this calculator, NRR appears automatically. If you see a number under 100%, click through to the SaaS Churn Calculator to pinpoint exactly how much revenue you’re losing each month and where the biggest friction sits.

The Rule of 40: When Does It Apply to Bootstrapped Founders?

The Rule of 40—originally a venture capital yardstick—states that your growth rate plus profit margin should equal at least 40%. For a bootstrapped SaaS that values profitability over blitzscaling, the rule shifts. You likely aren’t chasing 100% growth, but you also can’t afford to run at a loss. A bootstrapped founder who grows at 15% per year with a 25% profit margin hits 40% comfortably—and that’s a sustainable, exit-worthy machine.

In the early stages, I usually tell founders to aim for a combined score above 20%. Why? Because $300k ARR with 30% growth and negative 10% margin still needs external cash to survive. A score of 20%—say, 15% growth and 5% profit—keeps you above water without diluting ownership. The calculator displays your Rule of 40 percentage if you enter your profit margin. Use it as a compass, not a dogma. If you’re burning to grow and your Rule of 40 is, say, 12%, it’s a red flag that your growth isn’t covering the operational deficit. You either need to boost MRR faster or trim overhead.

How Fast Should Your Bootstrapped SaaS Grow to Avoid a Down Round?

Bootstrapped founders don’t raise rounds the same way as VC-backed startups, but many eventually seek strategic angel investment or a small seed round to accelerate. If you’re considering that path, your growth rate becomes the single most scrutinized metric after churn. A common heuristic: to raise at an attractive valuation, you need to show 10–15% month-over-month MRR growth over 6–12 months, with NRR above 90% and a clear path to $1M ARR.

Let’s put dollars behind that. A bootstrapped B2B SaaS at $15k MRR that has been growing at 12% MoM for the last 4 months will be at roughly $23.5k MRR in 4 more months if the trend holds. That’s $282k ARR. An investor would look at the trajectory and likely assign a 6-8x ARR multiple, implying a valuation around $2M. If the growth rate drops to 5% MoM, the projected ARR is only $18.2k in the same period, cutting the multiple to 4x and the valuation to sub-$875k. That’s a 57% valuation haircut from a 7-percentage-point growth slip. This calculator makes those projections tangible; you can test scenarios by varying your MRR inputs while keeping expansion and churn steady to see how sensitive your growth metrics are to a single bad month.

The most dangerous assumption is that growth will continue linearly. When churn spikes—often due to a competitor’s launch or a bug—the growth rate can collapse in 30 days. That’s why pairing this growth calculator with a churn prevention plan (read our deep dive on why SaaS churn rate increases) isn’t optional—it’s survival.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is considered a healthy MRR growth rate for a bootstrapped SaaS?

For a bootstrapped SaaS under $1M ARR, 8–15% month-over-month growth is exceptional, 5–8% is solid, and 0–5% indicates a need to reassess acquisition or retention. Anything negative is a red alert. The absolute number depends on your niche, but the rule of thumb is: if your growth rate doesn’t cover your burn, your runway is shrinking.

Can I use this calculator if I have negative MRR growth?

Yes. The calculator handles negative numbers gracefully. If your current MRR is lower than the previous month, the growth rate will display as a negative percentage. The interpretation will flag this as a danger signal so you can take immediate action on churn or contraction.

What’s the difference between NRR and MRR growth rate?

MRR growth rate measures total revenue change, including new customers. NRR (Net Revenue Retention) isolates the behavior of your existing customer base: expansions and churn. A company can have high MRR growth from aggressive acquisition and still have NRR below 100%, meaning existing customers are shrinking or leaving. Bootstrapped founders should track both to avoid overreliance on new logos.

Is the Rule of 40 relevant if I’m not raising VC?

Absolutely. For bootstrapped founders, the Rule of 40 forces a discipline check. If your growth plus profit margin falls below 15–20%, you’re likely either overspending to grow or not growing enough to sustain the business. It’s a quick gut check that prevents you from unknowingly running a charity.
